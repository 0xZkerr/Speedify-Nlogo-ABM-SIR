01

Introduction

In late 2019, SARS-CoV-2 emerged in Wuhan, China and rapidly propagated through global airport networks, reaching pandemic status by March 2020. Unlike the H1N1 pandemic, which produced one dominant lethal wave before receding, COVID-19 established a pattern of successive waves, each partly driven by the emergence of new viral variants and the uneven rollout of vaccines worldwide.

"Unless a global vaccination is implemented, we will have continuous waves of infections."

By mid-2021, over 200 million confirmed cases and more than 4 million deaths had been recorded globally. Three levers govern the pandemic's trajectory: social distancing (reducing contact between susceptible and infected individuals), non-pharmaceutical interventions such as masking, and vaccination, the only durable mechanism for building population-wide immunity.

Most prior modelling work used compartmental ODE-based models (SIR, SEIR, etc.) that treat populations as continuous densities. This paper instead develops a spatially-explicit Agent-Based Model (ABM) in which individual people move through a simulated city, interact in houses, small shops, and large stores, and can transmit infection depending on local crowding. This lets researchers directly study how city geography, movement behaviour, and variant competition shape epidemic waves.

Two model variants are studied: a two-strain model examining the interplay of a normal and a more lethal/transmissible variant alongside vaccination; and a multi-strain model examining evolutionary competition among five variants differing in transmissibility and lethality.

Theoretical Basis

2.1 The Classical SIR Model

The backbone of the model is the Kermack-McKendrick SIR framework (1927), in which a population is partitioned into three compartments. In differential equation form:

Eq. 1: Classical SIR ODEs
dS/dt = −β · S · I / N
dI/dt =  β · S · I / N − γ · I
dR/dt =  γ · I
S = susceptible  |  I = infected  |  R = recovered
N = total population  |  β = transmission rate  |  γ = recovery rate

The classical model assumes homogeneous mixing, where every individual has equal contact probability with every other. This paper extends it with spatial structure and individual agents.

2.3 Contact & Infection Probability

When a susceptible agent arrives at a location, infection is resolved in two sequential steps. First, the probability of contact with an infected individual scales with local crowding:

Eq. 2: Contact Probability
Pcontact = Ninfected / Nmax
Ninfected = number of infected agents at that location
Nmax = carrying capacity of that location

If contact occurs, transmission is tested against the variant-specific transmission rate. When multiple variants co-exist at a location, the probability of being infected by variant i is weighted proportionally:

Eq. 3: Weighted Variant Selection
P(infected by variant i) ∝ nᵢ × βᵢ
nᵢ = number of agents at location carrying variant i
βᵢ = transmission rate of variant i
Total infection test: Pcontact × βᵢ × (nᵢβᵢ / Σ nⱼβⱼ)

2.4 Death & Variant Lethality

After 7 days of infection, an agent faces a daily death probability. For the more lethal mutant variant, this probability is elevated by an additive penalty δdeath:

Eq. 4: Effective Death Probability
Pdeath,eff = Pdeath + δdeath · 𝟙[variant = mutant]
𝟙[·] = indicator function (1 if mutant, 0 otherwise)
δdeath = additional lethality of the mutant strain

2.5 Immunity Waning & Vaccination

Both natural immunity (recovered, Sᵢ = 2) and vaccine-induced immunity expire after T days, returning the agent to susceptible status. Vaccination applies daily to susceptible agents at rate Pvac, beginning at tick 300 (approximating the December 2020–January 2021 campaign start).

Eq. 5: Average Transmission Rate (Diagnostic)
βav(t) = (1 / |I(t)|) · Σᵢ∈I βᵢ
I(t) = set of currently infected resident agents at time t
Tracks which variant is dominant; rises when lethal/high-β variants spread

Explanation of Constants

The table below lists every parameter used in the simulation, its symbol, the value used in the paper's primary scenarios, and its physical interpretation. Values reflect the corrected NetLogo implementation.

Transmission & Disease Parameters
β
Normal Variant Transmission Rate
0.20
Probability that contact between a susceptible and infected agent results in transmission. Estimated from early SARS-CoV-2 literature. Range: [0.2, 0.3].
β + δβ
Mutant Variant Transmission Rate
0.25
The more transmissible strain has δβ = 0.05 added. Reflects Alpha/Delta-like variants with higher viral loads and faster replication.
Pdeath
Base Daily Death Probability
0.01
Applied daily after day 7 of infection for the normal variant. Approximate infection fatality rate given the model's simplified age-homogeneous population.
δdeath
Additional Lethality (Mutant)
0.004
Added to Pdeath for agents infected by the mutant variant. Creates selective pressure: more lethal variants kill hosts faster, eventually losing ground to less lethal but equally transmissible strains.
1/γ
Maximum Infection Duration
14 days
Agents remain infectious for up to 14 days. If they survive, they transition to immune status. Matches WHO clinical estimates for COVID-19 infectious period.
death-day
Earliest Day Death Can Occur
7 days
Death probability is only tested after 7 days of infection, reflecting clinical observation that severe outcomes typically manifest in the second week.
Immunity & Vaccination
T
Immunity Duration
120 days
Both natural and vaccine-induced immunity last T = 120 days (~4 months), after which agents return to susceptible. This drives the recurring wave pattern even with vaccination. A second scenario tests T = 180 days.
Pvac
Daily Vaccination Probability
0.005 or 0.001
The high-rate scenario (1/200 ≈ 0.005) models Chile/Portugal-style campaigns. The low-rate scenario (1/1000 ≈ 0.001) models early Brazil (200–300k doses/day). Only the high rate meaningfully suppresses waves.
tvac
Vaccination Campaign Start
Day 300
Corresponds approximately to December 2020–January 2021 globally, assuming pandemic detection in February–March 2020 as t = 0.
Movement & Spatial Parameters
Pmov
Resident Movement Probability
0.6 / 0.5 / 0.3
Probability per step that a resident leaves home. 0.6 ≈ 40% isolation; 0.3 ≈ 70% isolation. Brazil averaged 30–40% isolation. 70% isolation was sufficient to stop spread without vaccination.
Pmov,visitor
Visitor Movement Probability
1.0 (fixed)
Visitors always move, visiting exactly 3 locations per day. This is independent of the resident slider, reflecting that visitors behave like tourists/commuters who have no reason to stay home.
K
Location Carrying Capacity
50 / 500 / 12
Small shops hold 50 agents; large stores 500; houses 12. Crowding directly scales Pcontact. Large venues are the highest-risk transmission environments.
Visitor (Importation) Parameters
vinf
Visitor Infection Rate
0.001 (1/1000)
1 in 1000 arriving visitors carries the virus. Models the baseline importation pressure from external travel, seeding new waves even after local spread subsides.
vmut
Mutant Variant Among Infected Visitors
1/90 ≈ 1.1%
Of infected visitors, roughly 1 in 90 carries the more transmissible/lethal variant. This introduces the mutant stochastically; some simulations see earlier mutant dominance depending on seeding luck.
tstay
Visitor Stay Duration
1 day
Visitors spend exactly one day in the city. They cannot die, recover, or be vaccinated during their stay. This matches the paper's assumption that transit visitors contribute to spread but are not tracked longitudinally.

Explanation of Test Settings

The paper tests several combinations of social isolation and vaccination rate. The table below maps each scenario to the slider values you should set in the NetLogo simulation, summarises what the model predicts, and notes what real-world situation it approximates.

A: Baseline (No Vax) 0.6 40% 0 N/A 120 FAILS Continuous waves; ~10% deaths; mutant eventually dominant (~50% of deaths) Brazil early 2021, unvaccinated cities
B: High Isolation (No Vax) 0.3 70% 0 N/A 120 STOPS Susceptible pop stays near 95–100%; infection fails to spread Early lockdown (March 2020, some EU countries)

D: High Vaccination 0.6 40% 0.005 300 120 STOPS Second wave visible, then rapid suppression; mutant contained; βav → 0.2 Portugal 2021, Chile 2021
E: High Vax, Long Immunity 0.6 40% 0.005 300 180 STOPS Similar to D but with slower waning; fewer mid-term reinfections Hypothetical 6-month immunity scenario
F: Multi-Strain Competition 0.55 45% 0 N/A 120 EVOLVES High-β variants (2 & 3) displace original by wave 2; lower-β variants fail to invade; βav permanently elevated Delta displacement of original Wuhan strain, mid-2021
G: Moderate Isolation + High Vax 0.5 50% 0.005 300 120 STOPS Faster suppression than D due to combined effect; demonstrates policy complementarity Israel early vaccination push (Dec 2020–Mar 2021)

The critical threshold: vaccination at 1/1000 per day has no meaningful impact on wave dynamics. Only at ≥1/200 per day does the immunised pool grow fast enough to outpace the 120-day waning window.

When running Scenario F (multi-strain), watch the beta-av monitor. You should see it start near 0.20, drop slightly during inter-wave troughs, then permanently stabilise above 0.20 after wave 2, the signature of high-β variant takeover. Less transmissible variants (4 and 5, β = 0.14) never establish themselves because they cannot out-compete the original strain, let alone variants 2 and 3.

06

Key Takeaways

💉
Vaccination Rate Is the Decisive Variable

The difference between 1/1000 and 1/200 daily vaccination probability is the difference between no effect and pandemic suppression. Speed matters more than eventual coverage; slow campaigns allow waning immunity to undermine the immune pool before it accumulates.

🔁
Waning Immunity Drives Periodicity

The ~4-month wave cycle observed in the model directly reflects the T = 120-day immunity duration. Even in vaccinated populations, immunity waning continuously refills the susceptible pool, enabling repeated waves, the core mechanism behind COVID-19's wave pattern from 2020 onwards.

🧬
Transmissibility Beats Lethality in Variant Competition

In the multi-strain model, high-transmissibility variants rapidly displace the original strain regardless of lethality. The less-lethal but equally transmissible variant (3) eventually outcompetes the more-lethal one (2), consistent with evolutionary theory predicting virulence loss over time, as seen with Omicron.

🏙️
Spatial Structure Changes Outbreak Dynamics

Because infection probability scales with local crowding (Pcontact = Ninf/K), large venues are disproportionate transmission hotspots. The model captures super-spreading events that purely compartmental ODE models cannot represent, a structural advantage of the ABM approach.

✈️
Importation (Visitors) Perpetuates Waves

Even when local transmission subsides, infected visitors continuously re-seed the population, preventing true elimination. This explains why island nations and isolated communities with strict border controls fared far better than open economies during the pandemic.

🌍
Vaccine Nationalism Is Self-Defeating

The model's visitor importation mechanism demonstrates mathematically what was observed empirically: unvaccinated populations anywhere continue to generate new variants that re-invade vaccinated populations elsewhere. Global equitable vaccination is not just an ethical imperative; it is the only epidemiologically stable exit strategy.

📐
70% Isolation Alone Can Stop Spread, But Is Unsustainable

The model shows that Pmov = 0.3 (70% isolation) halts transmission without vaccination. However, maintaining this level is socially, economically, and psychologically destructive, making it a poor long-term strategy compared to rapid vaccination.
